About These Resources

This page brings together resources for students, educators, and academic communities who want to engage with artificial intelligence in ways that remain ethical, reflective, and human-centered. My approach emphasizes that AI should support learning rather than replace thinking. These materials are designed to help learners and instructors use AI with intention, transparency, and critical awareness.

These resources are also informed by my ongoing work in human-centered AI, metacognition, online learning, and the Human-Centered AI Metacognitive Learning Model (HAIML).

AI Use Guidelines for Students

Artificial intelligence can be a useful tool for brainstorming, clarifying ideas, improving organization, and supporting revision. At the same time, students are responsible for remaining the primary author and thinker behind their work. AI should not replace original analysis, reflection, or academic accountability.

Students should always review, question, and revise AI-generated output before using it. AI tools can produce inaccurate information, oversimplified claims, fabricated citations, or biased language. Responsible use means verifying ideas, checking course expectations, and making thoughtful decisions about when and how AI is appropriate.

Strong AI use in education involves transparency, human judgment, and a commitment to learning. The goal is not to outsource thinking, but to strengthen it.

AI Use Guidance for Faculty

Faculty can use AI to support instructional design, idea generation, content organization, feedback planning, rubric development, and learning support. However, effective integration requires clear boundaries, purposeful design, and continued instructor oversight.

Human-centered AI use in teaching should preserve student agency, encourage active learning, and support reflection rather than passive dependence. Instructors should clearly communicate expectations for AI use, define acceptable and unacceptable uses in coursework, and model critical engagement with AI tools.

Faculty also play an important role in helping students understand that AI systems are not neutral. These tools reflect design choices, training data, and limitations that must be examined critically within educational contexts.

Human-in-the-Loop Principles

Human-in-the-loop learning keeps people central in the learning process even when AI tools are used. In educational settings, this means that students and instructors remain responsible for interpretation, judgment, revision, and decision-making.

Key human-in-the-loop principles include:

  • Use AI to support thinking, not replace it.
  • Evaluate outputs for accuracy, bias, tone, and relevance.
  • Revise and personalize AI-generated material before using it.
  • Maintain transparency about when AI contributed to the work.
  • Center human values such as fairness, care, accountability, and reflection.

Prompt Frameworks for Students and Educators

Effective prompting can help learners and instructors use AI more thoughtfully. Strong prompts are specific, purposeful, and reflective. Rather than asking AI to do the work, it is often more productive to ask AI to support a process.

Brainstorming Prompt

"Help me brainstorm three possible approaches to this topic and explain the strengths and weaknesses of each."

Clarification Prompt

"Explain this concept in simpler language, but keep the main academic meaning intact."

Revision Prompt

"Review this paragraph for clarity and organization. Do not change my main argument."

Critical Thinking Prompt

"What assumptions might be missing from this argument? What alternative perspectives should I consider?"

Metacognitive Prompt

"Ask me questions that help me reflect on how I reached this conclusion and where I may need stronger evidence."

Ethical AI Checklist

Before using AI in an academic or instructional setting, consider the following questions:

  • Is the use of AI allowed for this assignment, task, or course activity?
  • Am I still doing the core thinking and decision-making myself?
  • Have I checked the accuracy of the information provided?
  • Could this output contain bias, fabricated information, or misleading claims?
  • Have I been transparent about how AI was used?
  • Does this use support learning, or does it bypass learning?
  • Would I feel comfortable explaining my process to my instructor, students, or colleagues?

Metacognitive Reflection Prompts

Reflection is essential when working with AI. These prompts can help students and educators think more deeply about how AI is shaping the learning process.

  • What did I use AI for, and why did I choose that use?
  • How did AI influence my thinking or decision-making?
  • What did I accept, revise, or reject from the AI output?
  • What did I learn through the process of engaging with AI?
  • Where did I need to use my own judgment most strongly?
  • Did using AI deepen my understanding, or make me less engaged with the task?
  • How can I use AI more intentionally next time?

Interactive Checkpoints for Learning with AI

These checkpoints can be used in courses, workshops, or self-guided learning to encourage ethical and reflective AI use:

  • Checkpoint 1: Before using AI, identify the learning goal. What are you trying to understand, improve, or create?
  • Checkpoint 2: During use, pause and ask whether AI is supporting your thinking or replacing it.
  • Checkpoint 3: After receiving output, review it critically for accuracy, bias, and relevance.
  • Checkpoint 4: Revise the output in your own words and based on your own judgment.
  • Checkpoint 5: Reflect on what you learned from the process and how your thinking changed.

AI Integration Strategies for Teaching and Learning

Thoughtful AI integration should be aligned with learning goals, disciplinary context, and ethical expectations. In educational settings, AI can be used to:

  • Support brainstorming and idea generation
  • Strengthen writing through revision and feedback
  • Encourage deeper reflection through questioning
  • Model critical analysis of AI-generated content
  • Help students compare human and AI reasoning
  • Create opportunities to discuss authorship, bias, and accountability

The most effective uses of AI are those that preserve active learning, encourage inquiry, and help students better understand both the possibilities and limitations of these tools.

Connecting AI Use to Human-Centered Learning

AI in education should not be reduced to efficiency alone. It should also be evaluated in terms of how it affects agency, reflection, critical thinking, identity, and trust. A human-centered approach asks not only what AI can do, but what learners need in order to grow, question, and make meaning in environments where AI is increasingly present.

My work in this area emphasizes that reflective practice, ethical awareness, and thoughtful instructional design remain essential. When AI is integrated carefully, it can support learning in meaningful ways. When used uncritically, it can flatten thinking, obscure accountability, and weaken engagement. The challenge is to design for wisdom, not just convenience.
