HAIML at a Glance

  • Human-Centered: Students remain active decision-makers when using AI
  • Experiential: Learning includes direct engagement with AI tools
  • Metacognitive: Students reflect on how AI shapes their thinking and learning
  • Ethical: AI use is evaluated through responsibility, authorship, and judgment
  • Structured: AI use is guided through intentional design and clear expectations

The Human-Centered AI Metacognitive Learning Model (HAIML), developed by Catheryn Reardon, PhD, is a framework designed to guide how students learn with AI while maintaining human agency, critical thinking, and ethical responsibility. Rather than positioning AI as a replacement for learning, HAIML centers the human learner and asks how AI can support reflection, judgment, and intentional engagement.

HAIML is grounded in the idea that students should not simply use AI to complete tasks. They should also think critically about how AI influences their choices, their confidence, their revision process, and their understanding. In this way, learning with AI becomes both experiential and reflective.

Foundational Theories

HAIML is grounded in established research on self-efficacy, self-regulated learning, and metacognition. Drawing on Bandura's theory of self-efficacy, the model recognizes that students' confidence in their ability to learn and make decisions plays a critical role in motivation, persistence, and engagement (Bandura, 1997, 2001). HAIML supports this by structuring AI use in ways that allow students to experience guided success while maintaining control over their learning.

The model is also informed by theories of self-regulated learning, particularly the work of Zimmerman, which emphasizes that learners actively plan, monitor, and evaluate their own learning processes (Zimmerman, 2002, 2008). HAIML extends this idea into AI-mediated environments by helping students make intentional decisions about when and how to use AI, rather than relying on it passively.

Metacognitive theory further supports HAIML by emphasizing awareness of one's own thinking (Schraw & Dennison, 1994; Bjork et al., 2013). The model integrates structured reflection to help students examine how AI shapes their reasoning, where it supports understanding, and where human judgment remains essential.

HAIML also draws on dual-process theory, particularly Kahneman's distinction between fast, automatic System 1 thinking and slower, deliberate System 2 reasoning (Kahneman, 2011). In AI-mediated environments, students face a heightened risk of accepting AI outputs through automatic processing, treating generated responses as credible without critical evaluation. HAIML's structured reflection tasks function as deliberate System 2 interventions, prompting students to slow down and examine how AI has shaped their thinking before accepting its conclusions. This connection between metacognitive practice and dual-process theory positions HAIML as a framework that addresses not only educational habit but the underlying cognitive mechanisms that make AI influence both powerful and potentially deceptive.

The framework is further grounded in naturalistic decision making research, which examines how people make judgments under conditions of uncertainty, time pressure, and incomplete information (Klein, 1998). This body of work is particularly relevant for graduate students entering applied professional fields, including clinical psychology, organizational consulting, and policy work, where decisions carry real consequences. HAIML prepares students not only to learn with AI but to exercise sound judgment alongside it in high-stakes, real-world contexts where AI tools cannot be the final decision-maker.

Research on automation bias, the tendency for humans to over-rely on and under-scrutinize automated system outputs, provides a critical empirical foundation for HAIML's human-centered design principles (Parasuraman & Riley, 1997; Cummings, 2004). Studies consistently show that when people work alongside automated decision support, they frequently accept erroneous recommendations and reduce their own monitoring effort. HAIML directly addresses this risk by building structured skepticism, authorship, and independent judgment into the AI use process at every level.

Together, these theoretical and empirical foundations position HAIML as a framework that not only supports learning with AI, but strengthens the confidence, reflection, and responsible decision-making that professional practice demands.

Why HAIML Matters

As AI becomes increasingly embedded in education and professional practice, students need more than access to tools. They need frameworks that help them understand when AI is useful, when it may limit their thinking, and how to remain active decision-makers in the learning process. HAIML provides that structure by connecting use, reflection, and ethics.

This model is especially important in writing-intensive, reasoning-based, and decision-making contexts where students may be tempted to outsource thinking rather than deepen it. Research on automation bias consistently demonstrates that people working alongside AI systems tend to reduce their own monitoring effort and accept AI-generated outputs without adequate scrutiny, even when those outputs are flawed (Parasuraman & Riley, 1997; Cummings, 2004). HAIML helps shift AI use away from convenience alone and toward the metacognitive and ethical growth that both learning and professional practice require.

This is especially true for graduate students in applied fields. Research in naturalistic decision making shows that sound judgment under uncertainty requires active evaluation, not passive acceptance of system outputs (Klein, 1998). Students preparing for careers in clinical psychology, education, healthcare, or organizational settings will encounter AI tools in contexts where the stakes of uncritical reliance are real. HAIML builds the habits of mind they will need to navigate those environments with confidence and integrity.

The Three Layers of HAIML

Experiential Layer

In the experiential layer, students actively engage with AI tools in authentic learning tasks. This may include brainstorming, organizing ideas, generating feedback, analyzing responses, or comparing AI-generated material with their own thinking. The goal is meaningful interaction with AI as part of the learning process, not passive consumption of AI output, but structured engagement that surfaces the ways AI shapes the task itself.

Self-regulated learning theory emphasizes that learners develop competence through active, intentional practice rather than observation alone (Zimmerman, 2002). In AI-mediated environments, this means students must do more than use AI. They must notice what changes when they do. Carefully designed experiential tasks create the conditions for that noticing, making the metacognitive layer possible.

Metacognitive Layer

In the metacognitive layer, students reflect on how AI influences their thinking, confidence, judgment, and revision process. They consider questions such as: What did AI help me see? What did it make easier? What did it make me rely on too quickly? Where did I accept an output without fully evaluating it?

This layer is grounded in metacognitive theory, which emphasizes awareness of one's own thinking as a foundation for learning and self-regulation (Schraw & Dennison, 1994; Bjork et al., 2013). Dual-process theory adds an important dimension here. AI tools operate at the speed and surface fluency of System 1, meaning they are fast, coherent, and immediately plausible, making it easy for students to accept outputs without engaging the slower, more deliberate reasoning of System 2 (Kahneman, 2011). HAIML's structured reflection tasks function as deliberate System 2 interventions, prompting students to examine how AI has shaped their thinking before treating its outputs as their own conclusions.

Ethical Decision-Making Layer

In the ethical decision-making layer, students evaluate AI use through the lens of responsibility, transparency, authorship, fairness, and human accountability. Students learn to determine whether their use of AI supports learning and integrity and, in applied contexts, whether it supports the standards their profession requires.

Bandura's work on self-efficacy is particularly relevant here. Students who believe their own judgment is capable and meaningful are more likely to exercise it, rather than deferring to AI out of uncertainty or habit (Bandura, 1997). HAIML supports that belief by giving students repeated, structured opportunities to practice ethical reasoning in the presence of AI, building both the skill and the confidence to use it.

HAIML and Structured AI Use

HAIML works in close alignment with my four-level AI use guidelines. Together, these models help students understand both what kinds of AI use are allowed and how to think about that use more deeply.

Self-regulated learning theory holds that learners develop not just knowledge and skills, but the ability to monitor and adjust their own processes over time (Zimmerman, 2008). The four-level framework gives students a clear external structure; HAIML develops the internal capacity to navigate that structure with intention. As students move across the levels, from working independently to collaborating with AI to evaluating AI-integrated work, the metacognitive and ethical demands increase correspondingly. HAIML ensures that progression is not just technological but reflective.

This combination supports a human-centered approach in which students remain active participants in their own learning and develop intentional decision-making in AI-rich environments.

Applications in Teaching and Learning

HAIML can be applied across a wide range of educational settings, including online courses, writing-intensive assignments, discussion activities, reflection tasks, and AI-supported feedback environments. Its three-layer structure is flexible enough to inform a single assignment or an entire course arc, and scalable from undergraduate through graduate and professional training contexts.

The framework is particularly well-suited to graduate-level programs where students are developing not only disciplinary knowledge but professional judgment. In these contexts, the question is not simply whether a student can use AI effectively. It is whether they can exercise sound judgment about when AI is useful, when its outputs should be questioned, and when human expertise must take precedence. HAIML builds that judgment explicitly, rather than leaving it to chance.

In my own work, HAIML informs course design, structured AI assignments, reflective prompts, AI-supported grading with human oversight, and faculty conversations about responsible AI integration. It has also shaped curriculum development for decision-making courses that examine AI as both a tool students use and a subject they critically analyze.

Future Directions

HAIML continues to evolve as I study how students experience AI-supported learning and how educators can preserve human judgment in technology-rich environments.

Current directions include examining how the three layers function across different student populations and professional training contexts, and developing assessment tools that capture metacognitive growth alongside content-area learning. I am also paying attention to emerging research across AI development and educational psychology, which is beginning to arrive at shared conclusions about the value of explicit reasoning, human oversight, and structured reflection. That convergence suggests that HAIML's principles have relevance not only in the classroom but in the broader conversation about how humans and AI systems work together responsibly.

Future work will also address the evolving nature of AI tools themselves. As AI capabilities expand, the frameworks students need to navigate them must expand as well, not by lowering expectations for human judgment, but by raising them.
